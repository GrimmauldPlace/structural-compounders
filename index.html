Investment Framework · Workbook

A theory of structural compounding

Six investor frameworks, ranked by a three-axis weighting model — composite conviction scoring, eleven-step research workflow, sell discipline, position sizing, bottleneck test, failure log.
By Will Dowd · Version 5.4 · April 2026
Prologue

My investment philosophy

I invest in businesses that either already own a structural position the world cannot function without, have crossed the threshold from brand into cultural institution that people incorporate into their own identity, or serve as the essential pipes through which entire industries must flow.

I only buy them, however, when the market has handed me a meaningful discount to their intrinsic value — the underlying worth of the business independent of what the market declares on any given day — because, as Warren Buffett said:

It is far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price.

— Warren Buffett

I also reserve the right to recognize when a disruptor is moving with such velocity and inevitability toward one of those three destinations that waiting for arrival means missing the journey, because the most permanent empires in business history all looked like competitors once before they became the only choice.

My edge is seeing that trajectory early enough to matter while maintaining the discipline to distinguish genuine inevitability from temporary momentum — and never confusing a great business with a great investment unless the price makes it both simultaneously.

Part I
The theory
Philosophy, frameworks and foundations

How to use this workbook

Organized to mirror how serious investment decisions actually unfold — from philosophy to framework to candidate identification to verification to action. Each section builds directly on the one before it.

Part I establishes the intellectual foundation. The investment philosophy defines what you are looking for. The six investor frameworks define the lenses through which you evaluate candidates. The common threads reveal what all six frameworks agree on. The reading list tells you where to go deeper. Read Part I end to end once, then return to specific investor chapters when a screen surfaces a relevant candidate.

Part II is the operating manual. The screeners surface raw candidates. The weighted conviction system ranks them by a methodology derived from documented track record, academic empirical support and philosophy alignment. The eleven-step research workflow tells you exactly how to verify a candidate before committing capital. Run Part II every time a new name appears in a screen.

Part III is the discipline layer. It exists because good entry decisions can still be undone by poor exit decisions, wrong sizing or the failure to learn from mistakes. Apply Part III before initiating a position and consistently after closing one.

The logical chain
  1. Philosophy defines what you are looking for.
  2. Frameworks give you six independent lenses to evaluate it.
  3. Screeners surface the raw candidates.
  4. Conviction scoring ranks them by weight of evidence.
  5. Research workflow verifies before you commit capital.
  6. Discipline layer governs how you act on and exit positions.
  7. Failure log closes the loop — the framework updates from evidence.

Preface: research methods

Each investor framework has both a practitioner tradition and an academic literature. The most useful academic material lives at the intersection of the investor's methodology and an empirical test of that methodology. When searching academic databases, construct queries that combine the investor or methodology as a subject term with a second concept — the specific factor being tested or the market being studied.

Example search constructions applicable across JSTOR, SSRN, NBER and Google Scholar

  • Buffett: combine "Berkshire Hathaway" with "quality factor" or "low beta"
  • Graham: combine "net current asset value" or "NCAV" with "returns"
  • Lynch: combine "PEG ratio" with "growth investing"
  • Fisher: combine "scuttlebutt" or "qualitative analysis" with "long-term returns"
  • Smith and Akre: combine "quality factor" or "return on capital" with "compounding"

Where a source is a popular-press book rather than academic literature, it is noted explicitly in the framework sections below. The goal is not to find validation — it is to understand where each framework has been tested, where it held up and where it has not.

The six investor frameworks

Presented in order of their composite conviction weight — highest first. Read the frameworks that carry the most predictive weight first. When time is limited, the Buffett, Smith and Akre chapters are the priority.

30%
Framework 1

Warren Buffett

In one sentence

Buy a wonderful business at a fair price. Never confuse a great business with a great investment unless the price makes it both.

Core investment philosophy

Warren Buffett describes himself as "85 percent Graham and 15 percent Fisher," though by the mid-1970s the Fisher share had grown considerably. The mature Buffett framework is built around a few durable ideas. A stock is a fractional ownership stake in a real business, not a ticker to be traded. The right holding period is "forever." The market, personified as Mr. Market, offers quotes daily that the investor is free to ignore; price is what you pay, value is what you get.

Buffett's later emphasis on durable competitive advantage — moats — shifted Berkshire's portfolio from Graham-style cigar butts toward high-return franchises such as See's Candies, Coca-Cola and American Express. The emphasis on owner-oriented management and rational capital allocation is arguably his most distinctive contribution to investment thinking.

Key screening criteria

  • Durable competitive advantage — pricing power, brand, network effects, low-cost position or regulatory moat.
  • Return on equity consistently above 15 percent without material leverage.
  • Earnings predictability across at least one full economic cycle.
  • Low capital intensity — businesses that don't require heavy reinvestment to grow.
  • Management that is candid, owner-oriented and rational about capital allocation.
  • Purchase price below a conservative estimate of intrinsic value, with a margin of safety.
  • Conservative balance sheet; debt used sparingly.

Most important books

  • Berkshire Hathaway shareholder letters, 1965 to present. The primary text — free at berkshirehathaway.com.
  • "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville," Hermes, 1984. Buffett's empirical defense of value investing.
  • "The Snowball" by Alice Schroeder (2008). The authoritative biography.
  • "Poor Charlie's Almanack," Peter Kaufman, ed. (2005).

Academic support and critique

Frazzini, Kabiller and Pedersen, "Buffett's Alpha" (Financial Analysts Journal, 2018): when returns are decomposed against Fama-French factors plus betting against beta and quality minus junk, Buffett's alpha becomes statistically indistinguishable from zero. The paper is usually cited as supportive — it tells us Buffett's approach is replicable in principle but hard in practice because stable, cheap, permanent leverage is rare.

25%
Framework 2

Terry Smith

In one sentence

Buy good companies. Don't overpay. Do nothing. Cash conversion is the lie detector of financial statements.

Core investment philosophy

Terry Smith founded Fundsmith in 2010. Fundsmith's philosophy reduces to three rules: buy good companies, don't overpay, do nothing. By good, Smith means companies that consistently generate high returns on operating capital employed — 20 percent or more — convert most of their accounting profits to cash, have durable competitive moats and fund their growth from retained cash flow rather than equity or debt issuance.

The "do nothing" rule reflects the empirical observation that trading costs and tax drag consume most of the theoretical benefit of active reallocation when the underlying portfolio already consists of high-quality compounders.

Key screening criteria

  • Return on capital employed above 20 percent, sustained across a decade.
  • Cash conversion — free cash flow equal to 90 to 100 percent of net income.
  • Gross and operating margins that rank in the top quartile of the industry.
  • Low capital intensity; limited need for acquisitions or heavy reinvestment.
  • Resilience through economic cycles — tested by looking at results in 2001, 2008 and 2020.
  • Conservative balance sheet and limited financial engineering.
  • Valuation that is reasonable relative to the quality.

Most important books

  • "Accounting for Growth" (1992). Smith's analysis of accounting games.
  • "Investing for Growth" (2020). An anthology of his Fundsmith shareholder letters.
  • Fundsmith annual owner's manual and letters at fundsmith.co.uk.

Academic support and critique

The quality factor — high ROIC, high ROE, low debt — underpins Smith's screening criteria and has the strongest academic backing in this workbook. Primary sources: Novy-Marx, "The Other Side of Value" (Review of Financial Studies, 2013) and Asness, Frazzini and Pedersen, "Quality Minus Junk" (Review of Accounting Studies, 2019). Both available through JSTOR.

20%
Framework 3

Chuck Akre

In one sentence

Compounding is the goal. The reinvestment moat is the engine. A business that redeploys retained earnings at high rates indefinitely is categorically different from one that plateaus.

Core investment philosophy

Chuck Akre founded Akre Capital Management in 1989. He describes himself as a compounding investor and his framework is best known as the three-legged stool. The image is deliberate: a three-legged stool cannot wobble on uneven ground the way a four-legged stool can. All three legs must be present simultaneously for the investment to stand firm.

Valuation is a prerequisite but not a central screen. Akre prefers to pay a fair price for a compounder and hold it for decades rather than trade in and out based on short-term multiple compression.

Key screening criteria — the three legs

  • Extraordinary business: durable and predictable return on equity and free cash flow. The business generates more capital than it consumes consistently across cycles.
  • Talented, honest management: skin in the game, long tenure, compensation aligned with long-term shareholder value and a track record of rational capital allocation.
  • Reinvestment moat: the ability to redeploy retained earnings at the same high rates the company already earns. Without this, a great business becomes a cash cow; with it, a compounding machine.

Most important books

  • Akre Capital Management quarterly letters at akrecapital.com.
  • "The Art of (Not) Selling" (2020) — why selling a true compounder is usually an error.
  • "100 Baggers" by Christopher Mayer (2015). Built on the same reinvestment-rate math.

Academic support and critique

The same quality factor literature supporting Smith supports Akre. The reinvestment moat concept is additionally supported by McKinsey's Valuation research (Koller, Goedhart and Wessels, 7th edition 2020) linking long-run value creation to return on incremental invested capital multiplied by the reinvestment rate. No significant academic challenge to Akre's three-leg framework exists.

15%
Framework 4

Philip Fisher

In one sentence

Own a small number of outstanding companies for decades. The most important information about a business never appears in its financial statements.

Core investment philosophy

Philip Fisher is often called the father of growth investing. Where Graham was quantitative and accounting-driven, Fisher was qualitative and management-driven. His thesis is that extraordinary long-term returns come from owning a small number of outstanding companies for decades. Price matters less than the business, and selling a great company because it has gone up too much is usually a mistake.

His signature technique is scuttlebutt: gathering evidence about a company's quality by interviewing competitors, suppliers, customers, former employees and trade contacts. The most valuable information is not in SEC filings and cannot be reverse-engineered from numbers.

Key screening criteria

  • Products or services with a large enough market to support years of growth.
  • Management committed to developing new products as the current line matures.
  • Research and development effective relative to company size.
  • Above-average sales organization.
  • Worthwhile profit margin, with active work to maintain or improve it.
  • Outstanding labor and personnel relations.
  • Depth of management below the top layer.
  • Strong cost analysis and accounting controls.
  • A long-range rather than short-range profit outlook.
  • Growth financed without frequent equity issuance that dilutes shareholders.
  • Management candor during difficult periods, not just good ones.
  • Management of unquestionable integrity.

Most important books

  • "Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits" (1958). The foundational text.
  • "Conservative Investors Sleep Well" (1975). Expands the 15 points.

Academic support and critique

Asness, Frazzini and Pedersen's "Quality Minus Junk" (2019) formalizes many Fisher traits as a composite quality factor. Novy-Marx (2013) shows that gross profitability is a priced factor. The main limitation: no peer-reviewed replication of the 15-point method exists as a direct test. Fisher managed private accounts from 1931 until his retirement in 1999 — no independently auditable public performance record exists, which is a genuine gap disclosed rather than papered over.

7%
Framework 5

Benjamin Graham

In one sentence

Buy at a meaningful discount to intrinsic value. Mr. Market will always offer you a price. You are never obligated to take it.

Core investment philosophy

Benjamin Graham is the intellectual founder of systematic value investing. Three concepts define his framework. Intrinsic value is the present value of a business's future earning power, estimated conservatively from tangible data. Margin of safety is the discount between intrinsic value and purchase price, which absorbs forecasting error and bad luck. Mr. Market is the allegorical partner who offers daily quotes driven by emotion rather than fundamentals.

In this workbook Graham functions primarily as a balance sheet safety floor — a 7 percent weight reflecting its value as a sanity check rather than a primary conviction driver. A business can be cheap because it deserves to be cheap.

Key screening criteria

  • Adequate size — annual sales above roughly $500 million today.
  • Strong financial condition — current ratio at least 2.0; long-term debt less than net current assets.
  • Earnings stability — positive earnings in each of the past 10 years.
  • Dividend record — uninterrupted payments for at least 20 years.
  • Earnings growth — at least 33 percent per-share growth over 10 years.
  • Moderate price-to-earnings — at most 15 on a three-year average earnings base.
  • Moderate price-to-book — at most 1.5. Graham accepts a combined P/E times P/B of 22.5.

Most important books

  • "Security Analysis" with David Dodd (1934; most recent edition 2008). The definitive technical treatise.
  • "The Intelligent Investor" (1949; 4th revised edition 1973). Buffett called chapters 8 and 20 "the two most important essays ever written about investing."

Academic support and critique

Oppenheimer's 1986 study found average annual returns of 28.3 percent from Graham's net-net strategy versus 10.7 percent for the benchmark. However, Fama and French's own 2020 paper "The Value Premium" acknowledges the premium has weakened in recent decades, particularly in the United States — a challenge coming from the framework's own academic champions.

3%
Framework 6

Peter Lynch

In one sentence

Invest in what you know. Find undervalued growth before Wall Street does. If you can explain the investment thesis in two minutes with a crayon, you understand the business.

Core investment philosophy

Peter Lynch ran Fidelity's Magellan Fund from May 1977 to April 1990 and compounded capital at roughly 29 percent a year — the strongest documented track record in this workbook. His philosophy rests on three pillars. First, invest in what you know. Second, understand the business model before you buy. Third, think like an owner with a long horizon.

Lynch carries the highest track record score in the three-axis weighting model but the lowest academic support score, because the PEG ratio — his primary metric — has been directly challenged in peer-reviewed literature. The 3 percent weight reflects that imbalance honestly.

Key screening criteria

  • PEG ratio at most 1.0 — Lynch called this fair value. At most 0.5 is a bargain. Above 2.0 is expensive.
  • Earnings growth sustained over at least five years, ideally 20 to 25 percent for fast growers.
  • Debt-to-equity at most 0.5; cash greater than long-term debt.
  • Inventory growth not outpacing sales growth — a classic warning sign.
  • Rising insider buying and stock buybacks.
  • Low institutional ownership — implies the stock is still undiscovered.
  • A simple, understandable story with a defensible niche.

Most important books

  • "One Up on Wall Street" (1989). The foundational text.
  • "Beating the Street" (1993). Case studies from the 1992 Barron's Roundtable picks.

Academic support and critique

Peters (1991) found positive risk-adjusted returns from buying low-PEG growth stocks. However, Voss, "The Fallacy of Price/Earnings to Growth Ratios" (Journal of Private Equity, vol. 14, no. 4, fall 2011, pp. 56–60) directly challenges PEG ratio validity, arguing it treats all growth as equally valuable regardless of capital requirements or competitive durability. Hoover and Sterbenz (Journal of Financial Education, vol. 29, spring 2003) also examine its limitations. Both available through JSTOR. Note that these critiques target the PEG metric specifically, not Lynch's broader approach of buying understandable growth at a reasonable price — an approach that draws indirect support from the quality factor literature cited above.

Common threads across the six frameworks

Reading the six frameworks side by side, seven consistent principles emerge. These represent the distilled consensus of the most successful investors in documented history.

PrincipleWhat it means in practice
Stocks are ownershipA share is a fractional claim on a real business — not a ticker to trade.
Long holding periodsFisher, Buffett, Smith and Akre argue for decade-plus horizons.
Margin of safetyGraham formalized it as a price discount. Buffett retains the phrase. Smith and Akre reframe it as business quality deep enough to absorb mistakes.
Return on capitalBuffett, Smith and Akre screen on it explicitly. Fisher's 15 points imply it. Only Graham's defensive framework tolerates low-ROE stocks.
Management qualityExplicit in Fisher, Buffett, Akre and Smith. Implicit in Lynch's preference for insider buying.
Skepticism of consensusMr. Market is Graham's metaphor and it animates the whole tradition. The quoted price can be wrong, often for long stretches.
Cash flow over profitSmith's cash-conversion test is most explicit. Buffett's owner earnings, Akre's free cash flow and Graham's dividend history are versions of the same principle.

Graham sets the floor: a quantitative framework that works even without qualitative edge. Fisher supplies the ceiling: a qualitative framework for identifying the small number of businesses that could compound for decades. Buffett is the synthesis. Lynch is the translator. Smith is the modern discipline. Akre adds the reinvestment dimension.

Weighted conviction system

Not all six frameworks carry equal predictive power. A company passing Lynch and Graham is a very different signal than one passing Buffett and Smith. Each framework is scored on three independent axes, averaged and normalized to produce a final weight. The weights are derived from a systematic process, not from opinion.

Note on Akre vs. Smith

Smith and Akre produce identical raw averages of 4.67. Akre is weighted lower at 20% vs. Smith's 25% because the reinvestment moat criterion requires qualitative judgment that is harder to apply consistently than Smith's quantitative cash conversion and ROCE tests. A case can be made for reversing these weights. This is the one weighting decision in the framework that a reasonable analyst could reach differently.

Note on Fisher

Fisher's 15% weight is arguably generous given the absence of any auditable track record (axis 1: 2/5). The weight reflects the qualitative depth of the framework and its philosophy alignment. If verifiable performance data surfaces that is weak, this weight should be reconsidered.

The Graham paradox

Graham ranks lowest in predictive weight despite being the intellectual father of the entire tradition. Buffett learned margin of safety and intrinsic value from Graham — then evolved the framework by adding the moat dimension under Munger's influence. Graham catches cheap stocks. Buffett catches wonderful businesses that happen to be cheap. The original genius is irreplaceable. The evolution is more powerful.

Research workflow — eleven steps

Two to three hours per company done properly. That is not a lot of time relative to the money you are putting to work.

  1. 01

    Run all six screens

    Identify every candidate that appears. Record name, screens passed and which specific screens.

  2. 02

    Run the composite conviction calculator

    More precise than counting screens. Composite score out of 100 determines position size range.

  3. 03

    Seven-question scorecard

    Five original questions plus stickiness gate (score 1 to 10, minimum 6) and pricing power gate (yes/no with evidence). See detail below.

  4. 04

    Three-scenario valuation sensitivity

    Conservative, base and optimistic. If the conservative case shows negative margin of safety, do not buy regardless of base case.

  5. 05

    Cyclicality check

    If the industry is cyclical, normalize earnings before trusting trailing metrics. Peak-cycle ROE flatters the screen.

  6. 06

    3 to 5 years of 10-Ks

    Read in order: Business — Risk factors — MD&A — Financial statements — Notes. The notes are where accounting quality issues surface.

  7. 07

    Last 4 earnings calls

    Watch for guidance changes and tone shifts. What is management emphasizing? What are they not mentioning?

  8. 08

    Firm research — minimum 2 reports

    Value Line, Argus, CFRA, Morningstar. See the firm research guide below.

  9. 09

    Portfolio cross-reference

    Total sector exposure across both IRA and taxable. No single stock above 10% at cost. No single sector above 35%.

  10. 10

    Sell discipline check

    Is the urge to sell coming from the business or the price? Write the answer before acting.

  11. 11

    Size the position

    Composite score maps directly to target position size using the conviction-to-size table above.

Step 3 detail — seven-question scorecard

Answer all seven questions before proceeding. Write the answers down. The discipline of committing to written answers is as important as the answers themselves.

  • Do I understand what the business does and how it makes money?
  • Is debt manageable? (D/E at most 0.5 is clean. Above 1.0 needs explicit justification.)
  • Is the pullback from the 52-week high explainable? (Down big = opportunity or broken?)
  • Is growth estimated or proven? (Check actual historical earnings, not just analyst forecasts.)
  • Is there a reason the market is underpricing it? (Too small? Too boring? Misunderstood?)
Question 6 — Stickiness gate

What is the realistic cost of a client switching away from this business — financially, operationally and regulatorily?

If the answer is catastrophic rather than merely expensive: stickiness is genuine. If the answer is inconvenient: stickiness is preference-based, not structural.

Apply the bottleneck test (Part III) before scoring.

Minimum acceptable: 6
Question 7 — Pricing power gate

Has this business raised prices consistently over the past decade? Did volume hold or grow through those increases? Can management raise prices tomorrow without losing customers?

If all three answers are yes: pricing power is structural, not cyclical.

Yes · No

If you cannot answer all seven, do more research before proceeding.

Step 8 detail — firm research guide

Firm research reports give you a trained analyst's thesis, price target and risk assessment as a cross-check against your own work. Each firm has specific strengths and specific blind spots. Understanding what each report is built on makes you a better consumer of it.

Minimum: two reports from different firms on every position before initiating. When two firms agree, conviction goes up. When two firms disagree sharply, understand why before investing.

The discipline layer
Sell, size, verify and learn

Sell discipline

The buy process has eleven rigorous steps. The sell decision deserves equal structure. Most investors hold too long when the thesis is broken and sell too early when the price is merely uncomfortable. The discipline is in telling the difference.

The diagnostic question — write this down before acting

Is the urge to sell coming from the business or from the price?

Business change  =  thesis broken  =  sell is rational.
Price decline  =  Mr. Market fearful  =  hold or add.

These sound similar. They are not. One is process. The other is fear.

Real sell signals — any single one warrants review; two or more warrant action

  • The competitive moat has narrowed — a credible competitor has emerged or switching costs have declined.
  • Management quality has deteriorated — capital allocation has become irrational or candor has dropped.
  • The business model has structurally changed in a way that undermines the original thesis.
  • Gross margins shrinking consistently across multiple quarters — earliest signal of pricing power erosion.
  • Free cash flow has diverged persistently from reported earnings — accounting quality concern.
  • Debt has grown to levels that threaten financial flexibility in a downturn.
  • The regulatory environment has permanently narrowed the addressable market or pricing power.

Not real sell signals — these are price events, not business events

  • The stock is down and it feels painful.
  • Macro headlines are negative — rates, tariffs, recession fears.
  • Sector rotation has pulled the stock down alongside unrelated peers.
  • An earnings quarter missed consensus but the underlying business trend is intact.
  • The stock hasn't moved and feels like dead money.

The harvest sell — when fair value has been reached

  • Price has converged with or exceeded intrinsic value across multiple frameworks.
  • A superior risk/reward opportunity has been identified through the framework.
  • Tax treatment is long-term (held over 12 months).
  • Redeployment capital has a specific, researched destination — not just cash.

If all four are true: harvest is rational capital allocation, not abandonment. Trim half, hold half when conviction in the long-term moat remains intact.

Cyclicality adjustment

Some businesses look exceptional on trailing metrics at the peak of their cycle and terrible at the trough. This check must be completed before trusting screen output on any cyclical business.

  • Does the industry have documented boom-and-bust cycles? (Energy, semiconductors, financials, housing and industrials are all cyclical.)
  • Has revenue varied by more than 20 percent peak to trough in any of the past three cycles?
  • Is current ROE or ROIC meaningfully above the 10-year average? (Possible cycle peak — be cautious.)
  • Is current ROE or ROIC meaningfully below the 10-year average? (Possible cycle trough — trailing screen may be too harsh.)
Normalization formula

Normalized earnings  =  average earnings across a full cycle (peak and trough combined)
Normalized P/E  =  Current Price / Normalized EPS

If normalized P/E passes but trailing P/E fails: business may be at a trough. Trailing screen is misleading. Investigate further.

If trailing P/E passes but normalized P/E fails: business may be at a peak. Apply extra margin of safety.

Bottleneck test

Stickiness describes how hard it is to leave. The bottleneck test asks something more specific and more rigorous: is this company merely participating in an ecosystem, or is it the route that others increasingly must use?

A strategic bottleneck requires two things simultaneously: a unique solution to an underlying technical or operational constraint, and control over access to that solution. Stickiness without bottleneck control is valuable but vulnerable. Stickiness with bottleneck control is the stronger position.

Bottleneck test — answer both before scoring stickiness
  1. Does this business solve a real constraint that others in the system cannot easily work around?
  2. Does this business control access to that solution — not just provide it, but own the chokepoint?
Both yes: score can reach 9 or 10.
First only: stickiness is real but capped — score 6 to 8.
Neither: stickiness is preference-based, not structural — score below 6. Investigate carefully.
Important caution

Morningstar tracks moat trend as positive, stable or negative — meaning competitive position can strengthen or deteriorate over time. A bottleneck confirmed at purchase is not a permanent fact. The wall in the rubber band analogy can move. Not often, but it can.

Disruptor exception — five-front evidence checklist

The framework allows early positions in businesses whose moat is still forming, sized at most 0.5 percent. Verify all five of the following before initiating any speculative position.

  • Bottleneck formation: solving a real system constraint or increasingly controlling access to the solution — not merely growing quickly inside a competitive layer.
  • Hardening network effects: the product is becoming more useful as participation grows, and the market structure is moving toward concentration rather than fragmentation.
  • Falling multihoming willingness: users, suppliers or complementors are finding it harder or less attractive to spread activity across competing platforms.
  • Economic proof, not just narrative proof: pricing power and unit economics are improving as the network matures. Strong network effects should ultimately produce better margins, not just a bigger top line.
  • Story-to-numbers discipline: the bull case can be translated into explicit assumptions about market size, share, margins, reinvestment, risk and realistic failure probability. If the thesis cannot survive that translation, it is narrative, not analysis.

Quarterly moat trend check

Buying a business with a strong moat is the entry decision. Verifying that the moat is still intact is an ongoing obligation. After reading each quarterly earnings call transcript for a core holding, answer these four questions in writing.

  • Is the chokepoint still owned? Has any competitor moved meaningfully closer to displacing the bottleneck position since last quarter?
  • Is pricing power intact? Did the business raise prices in the past year? Did volume hold or grow through that increase?
  • Are excess returns still being defended? Is ROIC stable or expanding, or compressing quarter over quarter?
  • Is the moat trend positive, stable or negative? Assign one label to each core holding each quarter and note if it has changed.
Moat trend deterioration rule
  1. One consecutive quarter of deterioration = yellow flag.
  2. Two consecutive quarters = red flag. Investigate immediately.
  3. Three consecutive quarters = sell signal regardless of current price.

The business has changed. The thesis no longer holds. Act on the business, not the price.

End note — keeping the framework alive

The theory and the practice drift out of sync over time. Four habits keep them aligned.

  • Update screen thresholds periodically as market valuations change.
  • Reread one Part I chapter each month — the frameworks need revisiting because markets change how they price things even when the underlying business logic does not.
  • Keep the master watchlist under 15 names — a longer list signals the screens are too loose or the scorecard is being skipped.
  • Review the failure log quarterly — a framework that never updates in response to its own mistakes is not a living document.
